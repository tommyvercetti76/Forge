# Family: Elapidae (cobras, kraits, mambas, sea snakes)

**Inherits from:** [_orders/serpentes.md](../_orders/serpentes.md)
**Inherited by species:** cobra (Indian cobra, *Naja naja*); future: king cobra, common krait, banded krait, sea snake

## 1. Family-universal anatomical features (deltas from Serpentes)

Elapids are **front-fanged venomous snakes**. Distinguishing family features:

- **Hood (cobras only — subfamily Najinae):** Expandable neck flap formed by elongated cervical ribs flaring outward when threatened or displaying. **THE iconic feature of cobras.** Spread hood looks like a flattened disc behind the head, with the head facing forward at the center.
  - The hood has the iconic **"spectacle" or "V" marking** on the back (visible from rear or 3/4 angle) — not seen in pure front view.
- **Fangs:** Short, fixed (proteroglyphous), at the FRONT of the upper jaw. Render mouth closed by default to avoid hallucinated fang display.
- **Head shape:** **Rounded** (slightly oval), **not triangular** like vipers. This is a key folk-art distinction — viper-render with elapid-marking is anatomically wrong.
- **Eyes:** **Round pupils** (NOT vertical-slit). Cobras have ROUND pupils — render as round dark dot inside the almond folk-eye.
- **Body proportions:** Long and slender. Indian cobra ~1.5-2 m. King cobra (when added) ~4 m.
- **Coloration:** Variable — Indian cobra has many "color morphs" (light brown, dark olive, jet black). The spectacle marking is more reliable than body color for identification.
- **Scales:** Smooth (NOT keeled like vipers). Rows along the body in regular dorsal pattern.

## 2. Sexual dimorphism (family-level)

**None visually.** Males and females are visually identical (males slightly longer in some species). Render cobras without sex specification. Photo references are 8 mixed-pose rather than sex-split.

## 4. Pose preferences (cited)

Elapidae pose vocabulary (refines serpentes):
- **spread-hooded** (**SIGNATURE**) — body coiled below, neck flat-spread, head raised + facing viewer. The cobra's defensive/display posture. Citations: Wüster & Thorpe (1989); Slowinski et al. (1997) for taxonomic-pose verification.
- **coiled-S-resting** — body in S-curve or loose coil, head NOT raised, hood NOT spread
- **glide-straight** — locomotion, body in flowing curves
- **strike-ready-coiled** — body coiled with head + neck raised, mouth closed but ready (rare pose; show only if action context)

**AVOID:**
- Standing on body
- Hood spread WHILE moving (cobras spread hood when stationary + threatened)
- More than one tongue
- Lizard-style head (triangular = wrong for elapidae)

## 5. Folk-art conversion (Mithila register, elapidae-specific)

- **Body fill color:** indigo `#1a2952` override.
- **Hood marking:** **Spectacle pattern preserved** — render the white-and-black "V" or "O" on the back of the spread hood. This is non-negotiable for cobra identity. Mithila tradition has a rich snake-iconography (Nāga); the spectacle marking translates naturally as a folk-medallion on the hood disc.
- **Scale pattern:** Translate as **regular folk-leaf or diamond pattern** following body curve. Lighter belly band visible.
- **Tongue:** **EXACTLY ONE forked tongue** (NEVER two — top failure mode). When tongue is extended, it's a single Y-shape with two prongs at the tip.
- **Eye character:** Round pupil inside almond folk-eye. Watchful + alert.
- **Head shape:** Rounded oval, NOT triangular.
- **Decoration density:** **balanced** (4 zones; over-decoration ruins the serpentine flow)
- **Required zones for elapidae:**
  1. Head crown medallion (small tikka above eyes)
  2. **Hood spectacle marking** (when hood spread) — the V-or-O pattern
  3. Body scale pattern (continuous, folk-leaf or diamond translation)
  4. Belly band (visible ventral side)
